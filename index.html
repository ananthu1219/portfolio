Mahatma Gandhi

The man who led millions with peace, not power.

A timeless legacy of truth and non-violence.

Biography

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi, born on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, Gujarat, was a lawyer, freedom fighter, and a symbol of peace and non-violence. Reverently called Mahatma (Great Soul), he led India's independence movement with truth and compassion.

He studied law in London and later moved to South Africa, where he faced racial discrimination. There, he began his journey as a civil rights activist and developed the philosophy of Satyagraha — non-violent resistance.

Returning to India in 1915, Gandhi became the leader of major movements such as the Non-Cooperation Movement, the Dandi Salt March, and the Quit India Movement. He inspired millions through his belief in non-violence, unity, and self-reliance.

He was tragically assassinated on January 30, 1948, but his life and legacy continue to inspire peace and justice movements around the world.

Philosophy

Ahimsa (Non-Violence)

Gandhi believed that non-violence is not a weakness but a powerful tool for change. Ahimsa guided all his actions — he resisted injustice without hatred or harm, even to the oppressor.

Satyagraha (Truth-Force)

Satyagraha was Gandhi’s method of fighting injustice through truth, patience, and nonviolent resistance. He believed that truth has the power to overcome any form of oppression.

Simple Living

Gandhi chose a life of simplicity, wearing hand-spun khadi, walking barefoot, and living in a self-sufficient community. He believed that materialism distracted people from spiritual growth.

Self-Reliance (Swadeshi)

He encouraged Indians to boycott foreign goods and support local industries. Spinning the charkha became a symbol of economic independence and national pride.

Religious Harmony

Gandhi respected all religions and fought against communal violence. He promoted unity between Hindus, Muslims, Christians, Sikhs, and others, believing that all paths lead to God.

Major Movements

Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-1922)

Gandhi launched the Non-Cooperation Movement urging Indians to withdraw from British institutions, schools, courts, and to boycott British goods. This mass protest marked the first large-scale, nationwide campaign for independence.

Dandi Salt March (Salt Satyagraha, 1930)

In protest of the British salt tax, Gandhi led a 240-mile march from Sabarmati Ashram to Dandi. The act of making salt from seawater became a powerful symbol of civil disobedience and inspired millions to join the freedom struggle.

Quit India Movement (1942)

During World War II, Gandhi called for the British to "Quit India" and grant immediate independence. The movement saw mass protests, strikes, and demonstrations across the country, despite severe repression by colonial authorities.

Champaran and Kheda Satyagraha

Gandhi led successful campaigns in Champaran (1917) and Kheda (1918) to address the grievances of farmers facing oppressive taxation and exploitation. These early victories established his leadership in the Indian independence movement.
